**What is *Transpersonal Psychology?***

*By Manal Al-Hammadi*

*Exploring the crossroad of human psychology and spiritual experience; a field that offers holistic therapy, integrative therapy, and the full depth of human consciousness.*

 

**Understanding the Field**

If you have ever felt that transitional therapy was missing something, that it speaks well to you behaviorally and cognitively but not to your sense of meaning, your spirituality, or your deepest questions about who you really are, then you are in a good company. That exact feeling is, in many ways, what gave rise to transpersonal psychology.

After two decades of studying how researchers and clinicians have tried to define this field, Glenn Hartelius arrived at a working description that has become widely cited: “transpersonal psychology is a transformative psychology of the whole person, situated within a diverse, interconnected, and evolving world, with particular attention to states of consciousness and developmental stages that expand beyond conventional notions of self” (Hartelius, 2022). That is a dense sentence, so let us break it down. At its heart, the transpersonal field acknowledge that human beings are not merely bundles of cognition and behavior (materialism). We are also spiritual beings capable of profound growth, connection, and transformation; and psychology as a discipline must take that seriously.

The word "transpersonal" means "beyond the personal". That does not mean overlooking the personal; the person history, emotions, daily struggles still matter deeply. Rather it means widening the lens so that experiences like transcendence, mystical experiences, peak moments, meditative awareness, and a felt sense of interconnection with something larger are treated as valid and important parts of being human. This wide view sits at the core of holistic therapy, which treats the whole person; body, mind, and spirit, rather than focusing on isolated symptoms and pathologizing extraordinary experiences of the human consciousness.

Many people first stumble across transpersonal psychology when searching for holistic counseling or asking what seems like a simple question: what is transpersonal psychology? Or perhaps traditional talk therapy helped with the surface issues but left something untouched. Maybe a spiritual experience “beautiful or bewildering” needed a space where it could be honored rather than pathologized. Whatever the entry points, what people tend to find in transpersonal psychology is a scholarly tradition that bridges Western clinical methodology with contemplative and wisdom traditions from around the world. It is a field where spiritual psychotherapy training sits alongside evidence-based practices, where the scientific and the sacred are treated not as opposites but as complementary ways of understanding the human condition and psyche.

Rodger Shute, writing from a philosophical standpoint, has argued that evidence from fields as diverse as physics, biology, and psychology points to what he calls a "transpersonal reality", a dimension of interconnected awareness that manifests across disciplines and that conventional materialism struggles to explain (Shute, n.d.). Whether or not one agrees with that framing, it captures something important: transpersonal psychology takes seriously the possibility that consciousness is more than a by-product of brain activity, and that our deepest experiences of connection, meaning, and transcendence deserve careful study.

 

*Transpersonal psychology was the first field within psychology to publish research on mindfulness and Eastern spiritual practices, the first to pioneer studies on the potential value of psychedelics, and the first to conceptualize spirituality as a human capacity separate from religion.*

\-        *International Journal of Transpersonal Studies, Aims & Scope*

 

**The History of Transpersonal Psychology**

The history of transpersonal psychology is the story of Western psychology slowly warming up to something that contemplative traditions understood long ago: the human psyche has dimensions and depths that cannot be captured by studying only behavior or pathology. Tracing how the field emerged helps us understand not just where it came from, but why the demand for transpersonal therapy continues to grow.

*The pioneers: James, Jung, and Assagioli \- 1901 – 1940s*

William James is often considered the grandfather of transpersonal psychology. His work The Varieties of Religious Experience (1902) argued that subjective spiritual experiences deserved serious psychological attention; a genuinely radical idea in a time where the field valued only the measurable. Remarkably, James used the term "trans-personal" in a 1905 lecture, long before anyone else picked it up in an academic context. Carl Jung, meanwhile, was developing his ideas about the archetypes, collective unconscious, and individuation; concepts that would become later on the pillars of transpersonal thought and essence. Roberto Assagioli, the Italian psychiatrist who created psychosynthesis, also laid the groundwork by explicitly including a spiritual dimension in his model of the psyche. Assagioli is often credited as one of the first to use the term "transpersonal" in a clinical context.

*The Hunger for Something More \- 1950s – 1960s*

Abraham Maslow, a central figure in humanistic psychology, grew increasingly fascinated by what he called "peak experiences"; moments of intense joy, unity, and transcendence that the existing frameworks could not fully explain. By the late 1960s, Maslow sensed that even humanistic psychology, with all its emphasis on self-actualization, did not go far enough in honoring the spiritual dimension of human potential. Together with Anthony Sutich and Stanislav Grof, he began working toward a "fourth force" in psychology, after psychoanalysis, behaviorism, and humanistic psychology (Grof, 2008). This period also saw growing interest in experiential practices like psychedelics as a catalyst for expansion and breathwork while other experiences were being explored seriously in clinical contexts.

*The Formal Formation – 1969 – 1990s*

In 1969, the Journal of Transpersonal Psychology published its first issue, with Maslow and Sutich as founding editors. The Association for Transpersonal Psychology followed in 1972, and the International Transpersonal Psychology Association was established in 1973\. For the first time, there was an organized institutional home, journals, associations, conferences, for studying spiritual experience, consciousness, and human growth with scholarly accuracy. This foundation made it possible for transpersonal psychology courses and education programs to develop over the following decades.

Over the following decades, the field witnessed significant expansion. Institutions such as the California Institute of Integral Studies, Naropa University, Saybrook Graduate School, and JFK University began offering dedicated transpersonal psychology degree programs. Stanislav Grof's research into non-ordinary states of consciousness; particularly through his development of Holotropic Breathwork, brought body-centered experiential methods into clinical practice and laid the groundwork for modern breathwork. Ken Wilber contributed a wide-reaching philosophical model mapping human development from pre-personal to transpersonal stages. In 1993, a new diagnostic category called "Religious or Spiritual Problem" was approved for inclusion in the DSM-IV; a real landmark that signaled mainstream psychiatry was beginning to acknowledge what transpersonal practitioners had been working with for decades. Through all of this, holistic psychotherapy programs deepened their emphasis on self-inquiry alongside clinical skill as non-negotiable asset for the transpersonal practitioners.

*The Second Wave \- 2000s – Present*

Perhaps the most important recent development is what scholars have called the "second wave" of transpersonal psychology. A large-scale bibliometric study by Hartelius and colleagues (2021), published in the Journal of Transpersonal Psychology, analyzed four decades of articles from the field's two principal journals. They found that the field has been gradually moving away from its original, narrower focus on altered states of consciousness toward a broader and more grounded orientation. This second wave puts bigger emphasis on understanding the whole person as part of larger living system, giving more weight to transformation rather than mere transcendence, and allows closer attention to embodiment, social context, ecology, and most importantly; diversity (Hartelius et al., 2021). David Furlong, in a 2024 paper in the Transpersonal Psychology Review, explored complementary landscape by examining how wave/particle duality in quantum physics might offer a framework for understanding transpersonal experiences of expanded consciousness; suggesting that consciousness itself may have both a bounded, "particle" state and an interconnected, "wave" state (Furlong, 2024). Meanwhile, renewed scientific interest in psychedelic-assisted therapy and the scientific acceptance of mindfulness-based interventions have brought many transpersonal ideas squarely into the mainstream. The demand for spiritual psychotherapy training and holistic therapy practitioners has never been more in demand.

 

**How Transpersonal Psychology Differs from Other Approaches**

One of the most common questions people ask, whether they are potential clients, students considering a transpersonal psychology program, or professionals exploring integrative therapy education; is how this transpersonal psychotherapy actually differs from other therapeutic modalities. The short answer is that it does not throw out what those modalities have built. It widens the conversation to include dimensions they generally omit.

Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) is structured, goal-oriented, and well-evidenced for conditions like depression and anxiety. But it generally does not explore the questions of meaning, spiritual experience, or deeper layers of consciousness. CBT is a great first aid tool; effective and fast, but if someone is in the middle of a deeper crisis; a profound experience that has shaken their sense of reality, the CBT framework is not built to hold that kind of material.

Psychoanalytic and psychodynamic approaches venture deeper into the unconscious, exploring early relational patterns and the hidden workings of the psyche. They can be extremely insightful. But they usually stop short of the transpersonal; the terrain of spiritual awakening, interconnection, and states that reach beyond the individual self. The only exception is Jung's analytical psychology; it is widely considered a direct ancestor of transpersonal psychology.

While humanistic psychology; person-centered therapy, Gestalt, existential approaches, shares transpersonal psychology's warmth, respect for the person, and emphasis on growth. In a matter of fact, transpersonal psychology emerged directly out of the humanistic field. But humanistic methods generally stop at the border of the individual self. Transpersonal psychology asks: what happens when we go further?

Hartelius's twenty-year definitional research identified three core themes that set the field apart: it is a psychology that explores self-expansion beyond the ego, it takes a whole-person and integrative approach, and it is primarily concerned with transformation (Hartelius, 2022). Rimban writing about the application of transpersonal principles in educational settings, highlights the field's distinctive emphasis on interconnectedness and on cultivating both consciousness and compassion, qualities that go well beyond what most other psychological frameworks prioritize (Rimban, 2024).

None of this means transpersonal psychology replaces CBT or psychodynamic work, far from it. A therapist trained in transpersonal psychotherapy training will often utilize multiple modalities, using cognitive techniques for anxiety management, relational depth for attachment wounds, and transpersonal tools for existential and spiritual dimensions. That flexibility is what makes transpersonal therapy so valuable, and it is exactly what a good transpersonal psychology program is designed to develop.

 

**Transpersonal Modalities**

Transpersonal modality still remains broadly defined, and wide range of therapeutic practices fall under the transpersonal umbrella. What unites them is a shared willingness to work with consciousness, spiritual development, and the deeper layers of lived experience. Many are studied within holistic counseling courses and transpersonal psychology programs, and they form the practical backbone of any serious integrative therapy education.

Importantly, the second-wave research by Hartelius et al. (2021) found that the field's center of gravity has shifted over time away from a narrow focus on altered states and toward broader engagement with embodiment, ecology, social justice, and whole-person transformation (Hartelius et al., 2021). The modalities below reflect both the field's original concerns and this newer, more grounded orientation.

*Holotropic Breathwork*

Developed by Stanislav Grof, this experiential modality uses accelerated conscious breathing technique, carefully chosen music playlist, and focused bodywork to access non-ordinary states of consciousness. It remains one of the best-known forms of breathwork for anxiety, emotional release, and deep self-exploration. It requires thorough practitioner training and careful participant screening.

*Mindfulness-Based Psychotherapy*

Deeply rooted in Buddhist traditions, mindfulness-based approaches cultivate present-moment awareness and compassionate observation of inner experience. Transpersonal psychology established research on these practices decades before mindfulness became a western cultural phenomenon. They are now central to holistic therapy and feature in virtually all transpersonal psychology programs and courses.

*Psychosynthesis*

Founded by Roberto Assagioli, psychosynthesis works with sub-personalities, the will, and the concept of a "higher self." It provides a structured map of the psyche that explicitly includes a spiritual dimension, making it a foundational approach in transpersonal psychotherapy training programs internationally.

*Jungian Analytical Psychology*

Although predated the formal transpersonal movement, Jung's work on archetypes, the collective unconscious, individuation, and the spiritual function of the psyche is profoundly transpersonal in character. Jung introduced the principle of trusting one's psychological process, implying that consciousness carrying integral tendencies toward growth and progression.

*Somatic & Body-Oriented Therapies*

Modalities such as somatic experiencing, Hakomi, and other body-centered approaches work with the felt sense of the body to release stored trauma and access deeper layers of awareness. The second wave of transpersonal scholarly work has underlined embodiment as a crucial dimension that was sometimes overlooked in earlier phases of the transpersonal psychology (Hartelius et al., 2021). These methods are increasingly fundamental part of the transpersonal education and practice.

*Ecopsychology & Nature-Based Therapy*

The second wave's emphasis on ecological context has elevated ecopsychology; the study that explores the interconnectedness between the human mind and psyche and nature. Hartelius documented a marked increase in ecology-related themes in transpersonal journals over recent decades (Hartelius et al., 2021). This perspective now enhances both transpersonal therapy practice and transpersonal counseling programs with an ecological dimension.

**Transpersonal Tools & Therapies**

Beyond the broader modalities above, transpersonal practitioners use a range of tools and techniques in their daily psychotherapeutic work. These tools are typically taught within the psychotherapy training programs and advanced courses; the aim is to deepen the therapeutic process in ways that talk therapy alone cannot achieve.

Breathwork is probably the most accessible and widely practiced transpersonal tool. The range is broad; from gentle diaphragmatic breathing for nervous system regulation to intensive practices like Holotropic Breathwork or rebirthing breathwork. For individuals struggling with chronic anxiety, panic, or stress-related conditions, breathwork offers a direct, embodied route to self-regulation that complements traditional psychotherapy. Importantly, it does not just manage symptoms; it can open a outlet into stored emotional material and, sometimes, into expanded awareness.

Dream work, rooted in Jungian and other traditions, treats dreams as meaningful communications from the unconscious; not random imagery, but signals directing toward unconscious material ready to merge into consciousness. Journaling and reflective writing promote self-awareness in a low-pressure, self-directed way. Art therapy and creative expression, like painting, sand-tray work, movement, music, bypass the noise of intellect and allow the psyche to speak through symbol and metaphor. These are very valuable tools for individuals who find it difficult to express their experiences with words, which is common in both spiritual and trauma-related work.

What matters is that none of these tools are used carelessly. Rimban (2024) highlights that integrating transpersonal tools into any setting demands considerate attention to the specific needs of the individuals and a strong ethical foundation (Rimban, 2024). Professionals trained through a transpersonal psychology learn to select and apply these tools and methods with clinical discernment, adapting to each individual specific situation rather than imposing a unified template.

 

**Clinical Considerations**

Like any therapeutic modality, transpersonal psychotherapy and its tools and methods are not the right fit for every person in every situation. Understanding when these approaches are likely to help and when they might do more harm than good is one of the most important things taught in any responsible psychotherapy training or course. Christina and Stanislav Grof's foundational work on "spiritual emergency" makes the point clear: experiences of non-ordinary consciousness – and that applies to any transpersonal dimension \- can be profoundly healing for some people and deeply destabilizing for others, depending on context, individual psychological profile, and quality of support available (Grof & Grof, n.d.).

*When Transpersonal Approaches Are Indicated*

*Existential and Meaning-Related Crisis*: People wrestling with questions of purpose, mortality, identity, or spiritual longing often find that transpersonal work speaks to them in ways symptom-focused therapies do not. Transpersonal therapy provides the framework needed to hold these profound explorations.

*Spiritual Emergencies and Awakening*: Mystical experiences, kundalini awakenings, or psychospiritual crisis can be frightening if misunderstood. A therapist with spiritual psychotherapy training can distinguish between a spiritual emergency and a psychotic episode; a distinction with serious clinical implications that traditional training often does not cover.

*Grief, Loss, and Life Transitions:* The transpersonal perspective honors the spiritual dimension of grief and change, offering ritual, contemplative practice, and a willingness to sit with the unknown; qualities that make transpersonal therapy especially suited to people navigating mourning, divorce, retirement, or other major life events.

*Anxiety and Stress-Related Conditions*: Techniques such as breathwork, mindfulness meditation, and somatic awareness practices can be highly effective for chronic anxiety, particularly when combined with a transpersonal therapy modality that addresses root causes of the suffering rather than solely managing symptoms.

*Post-Traumatic Growth*: Individuals who have alleviated trauma symptoms and seek deeper meaning in their experience, transpersonal modality can support a level of healing and integration that goes well beyond symptom reduction into personal growth and deepening life meaning and purpose.

*Personal and Spiritual Development:* The beauty of transpersonal work does not require a clinical diagnosis or pathology but allows those are committed to ongoing growth and self-knowledge to have the space for healing and growth without judgment. The field welcomes this, recognizing that the drive toward self-expansion is a healthy human impulse (Hartelius, 2022).

 

*When Caution or Alternatives Are Needed*

*Acute Psychosis or Severe Dissociation:* Transpersonal techniques involving altered states, intensive breathwork, deep meditation, can be destabilizing for people experiencing active psychosis, severe dissociation, or a fragile sense of self. In this case, psychiatric care needs to come first. This is not a failure of transpersonal work; it is a question of timing and readiness.

*Unresolved Acute Trauma:* While transpersonal modalities can support deep trauma integration over time, they are generally not the right initial point for someone in active crisis. Trauma-specific modalities like EMDR or somatic experiencing should be used first, with transpersonal work introduced once stability is established.

*Medical Conditions Requiring Screening*: Intensive breathwork practices require medical authorization for people with cardiovascular disease, epilepsy, respiratory conditions, or pregnancy. Responsible transpersonal psychology program teaches screening protocols before offering breathwork or other intensive experiential methods.

*Spiritual Bypassing:* This is one of the understated counterindications. "Spiritual bypassing" is the pattern of using spiritual practices to avoid confronting painful emotions, relational wounds, or personal difficulties. A well-trained transpersonal will recognize this tendency and gently redirect toward the personal material that needs attention.

*Inadequate Practitioner Training:* Transpersonal tools can open up powerful psychological territory. They should only be offered by people who genuinely know what they are practicing. This is exactly why choosing an accredited transpersonal psychology degree matters; it ensures competence, ethical grounding, and the capacity to hold space for whatever arises, no matter how difficult it is.

 

**Training in Transpersonal Psychology**

If this field speaks to you; if you feel drawn not just to helping people manage symptoms but to supporting sincere transformation; the path typically begins with a transpersonal psychology program that offers both foundational theory and experiential learning. From there, many students pursue a full transpersonal psychology postgraduate level education with accreditation, building the therapeutic competence and theoretical depth needed for professional practice.

What distinguishes the best transpersonal therapy education programs is that they do not simply teach about transpersonal psychology, they invite students into a direct, lived experience of it. Trustworthy training combines academic study with personal therapy, retreats, supervised clinical practice, and intensive workshops in specific modalities. The International Journal of Transpersonal Studies; the field's largest peer-reviewed publication, read in 185 countries and the only transpersonal journal listed by Scopus, describes transpersonal psychology as “dedicated to whole-person approaches that are empirically grounded while remaining open to the transpersonal dimensions of experience” (International Journal of Transpersonal Studies, n.d.).

When evaluating programs, look for those accredited by recognized professional bodies like the European Transpersonal Association (EURTOS), staffed by experienced faculty with active clinical practices, and structured around an open learning community. Consider whether the program requires adequate supervised hours, mandates personal therapy, and offers training in a range of transpersonal tools; including breathwork, meditation, somatic methods, and ethical frameworks for working with spiritual experience. The field's ongoing shift toward greater empirical rigor, documented in the bibliometric research of Hartelius, means that the best transpersonal psychotherapy training programs today are more scientifically grounded than ever, without losing what makes them distinctive.

A strong transpersonal psychotherapy school prepares you not just to help others, but to deepen your own understanding of what it means to be fully, richly human; with all the complexity, mystery, and potential that entails. 

**What to Look for in a Transpersonal Psychotherapist**

Finding the right transpersonal psychotherapist matters; perhaps more than in some other modalities, because the work can touch on the most vulnerable parts of who you are. Whether you arrived here through your own research, a friend's recommendation, or a growing sense that something was missing from traditional therapies, the following aspects are worth considering. A good transpersonal therapist embodies the principles of transpersonal therapy not just in sessions but in their own ongoing commitment to growth and self-examination.

1\.     Verified training and qualifications: Look for someone who has completed a recognized transpersonal psychology degree, an accredited transpersonal psychology school, or equivalent psychotherapy training. Accreditation from a professional body — such as EUROTAS, ensures observance to educational and ethical standards. Be cautious of people who claim transpersonal expertise without verifiable credentials.

2\.     A solid clinical foundation: The best transpersonal therapists do not float above the ground. They combine spiritual awareness with proper clinical skill; the ability to assess risk, work safely with trauma, and recognize when a referral is needed. Their integrative therapy education should be evident in their capacity to meet you wherever you are, not just where the transpersonal techniques are most interesting.

3\.     Their own personal practice: A transpersonal therapist should have an ongoing contemplative or spiritual practice; meditation, breathwork, personal therapy, supervision, or some combination. This is not optional. It is what gives the therapist the depth and presence to hold space for your process. Do not be shy to ask.

4\.     Ethical clarity and professional boundaries: A responsible transpersonal psychotherapist maintains clear boundaries, works within an ethical code, and is transparent about their methods and limitations. They should never push spiritual beliefs onto you or use their position to manipulate. Transpersonal therapy at its best is empowering, never dogmatic.

5\.     Cultural humility and inclusivity: The field draws on many spiritual and cultural traditions. The second wave has placed increasing emphasis on diversity and social context (Hartelius et al., 2021). Your therapist should approach this material with genuine respect and openness to your own background, rather than imposing a particular worldview.

6\.     A collaborative stance: The transpersonal therapeutic relationship is, at its best, a genuine partnership. Your therapist should welcome questions, respect your pace, and adjust based on your feedback. The best transpersonal psychotherapy education emphasizes this collaborative, client-centered ethic from the very beginning of training.
